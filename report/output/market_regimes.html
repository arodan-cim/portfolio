Market Regimes

How do macro environments map to portfolio component performance?

Markets cycle through four fundamental regimes defined by the intersection of growth vs stagnation and low vs high inflation. The Golden Butterfly is designed to hold assets that thrive in each quadrant.

The 2×2 framework (Dalio / Bridgewater "All Weather" model):
Each regime favors different asset classes. A balanced portfolio holds all four so that at least one component performs well in any environment.
Q1Growth + Low Inflation
Stocks up, bonds stable, gold flat. Best for: Total Market, SCV.
Q2Growth + High Inflation
Stocks mixed, bonds down, gold up. Best for: Gold, SCV.
Q3Stagnation + Low Inflation
Stocks down, bonds up, gold mixed. Best for: LT Bonds, ST Bonds.
Q4Stagnation + High Inflation
Stocks down, bonds down, gold up. Best for: Gold, ST Bonds.

Methodology

Each year classified via median split on YoY GDP growth and YoY inflation.

Thresholds: GDP median = 2.91%, CPI median = 2.92%

Data sources:

  • CPI: FRED CPIAUCSL (Bureau of Labor Statistics)
  • GDP: FRED GDPC1 (Bureau of Economic Analysis)

Component Performance by Regime

Distribution of annual metrics per component in each quadrant. Dashed line = mean. Proxy (solid) and ETF (dotted) shown side by side.

Q1 — Growth + Low Inflation (16 years)

The best environment for equities. SCV leads with +19.2% avg return (Sharpe 1.75), followed by Total Market at +16.9%. Both show low volatility (12.1%) and shallow drawdowns (-10.0%). Win rates are high: SCV 72%, TM 69%. ST Bonds deliver the best Sharpe (3.03) and Calmar (4.52) due to near-zero drawdowns, but returns are modest (+2.7%). LT Bonds underperform (+1.4%) — low inflation means no rate cuts to boost bond prices. Gold is flat (+0.6%) — no inflation hedge needed.

Q2 — Growth + High Inflation (21 years)

Gold dominates with +14.3% avg return (mean; median is 0% due to Bretton Woods pre-1971 fixed price), driven by massive spikes during inflationary episodes (1971–1979). Equities remain positive (TM +9.5%, SCV +11.7%) — growth supports earnings even as inflation erodes real returns. Volatility rises to 12.7% for equities. LT Bonds at +5.4% — rising inflation pushes yields up and prices down, with -14.4% avg drawdown. ST Bonds benefit from rising rates: +5.2% with 73% win rate.

Q3 — Stagnation + Low Inflation (22 years)

LT Bonds shine with +10.2% avg return — central banks cut rates during stagnation, boosting bond prices. However, volatility is high (15.3%) with -12.7% avg drawdown. Gold also performs well (+10.8%) as a safe haven. Equities are mixed: TM +8.4% (median +11.4%) with elevated volatility (16.4%) and deeper drawdowns (-15.8%). This is where the Golden Butterfly's bond allocation earns its keep. ST Bonds remain steady (+3.9%, 73% win rate).

Q4 — Stagflation (17 years)

The worst environment for most assets. Equities suffer: TM +3.5% (median -0.9%), with the highest volatility (16.0%) and deepest drawdowns (-16.9%). Win rate drops to 51% — barely a coin flip. LT Bonds also struggle (+6.0%, median -1.2%) with -21.3% avg drawdown — inflation destroys bond value. ST Bonds are the standout: +8.6% avg return, best Sharpe (3.68) and Calmar (7.36) of any asset in any quadrant. Win rate 78%. Short duration protects against rate hikes while capturing rising yields. Gold at +2.6% — modest, but the only other asset with positive mean return alongside ST Bonds.

Key Takeaways

  • Equities (TM, SCV) dominate in Q1, hold up in Q2/Q3, but suffer in Q4. SCV consistently outperforms TM by 2–3pp across all regimes.
  • LT Bonds are the Q3 hero but the Q4 villain. High volatility makes them unreliable outside their sweet spot.
  • ST Bonds are the portfolio's anchor: positive in every quadrant, best risk-adjusted returns in Q4 (when everything else fails). Low volatility, high win rate, minimal drawdowns.
  • Gold spikes in Q2 (inflation hedge) and provides diversification in Q3, but is unreliable in Q1/Q4. Pre-1971 data is flat (Bretton Woods).
  • The Golden Butterfly works because no single quadrant leaves all 5 components negative. In the worst regime (Q4), ST Bonds and Gold provide positive returns while equities and LT Bonds suffer.

Why Proxies Matter

Comparing the Proxy and ETF tabs reveals a critical limitation of ETF-only analysis. ETFs have existed for only ~20 years (US) or ~15 years (EU), and this period is heavily skewed toward Q3 (stagnation + low inflation — the post-GFC, QE era). The data coverage per quadrant:

QuadrantProxy yearsETF years (from 2001)Assessment
Q1164⚠️ Limited
Q2214⚠️ Limited
Q32214✅ Sufficient
Q4173⚠️ Limited

With only 4 Q1 years and 3 Q4 years in the ETF period, drawing conclusions about portfolio behavior in growth or stagflation regimes from ETF data alone would be statistically meaningless. The proxy chain extends coverage to 16 Q1 years and 17 Q4 years, capturing the full range of macro environments including the 1970s stagflation, 1980s disinflation bull, and 1990s tech boom — regimes that no ETF has ever experienced. This is the fundamental reason proxies are necessary: without them, the analysis would only reflect the post-GFC low-rate world, which is precisely the environment least likely to persist.

Methodology

Each year classified via median split on YoY GDP growth and YoY inflation.

Thresholds: GDP median = 2.03%, HICP/blend median = 2.96%

Data sources:

  • CPI 1956–1996: blend of DE/FR/UK/IT CPI (FRED, OECD MEI)
  • HICP 1996+: FRED CP0000EZ19M086NEST (Eurostat)
  • GDP 1971–1995: blend of DE/FR/UK real GDP index (FRED, OECD)
  • GDP 1995+: FRED CLVMNACSCAB1GQEA19 (Eurostat)

Pre-1956: US CPI used as EU inflation proxy. Pre-1971: US GDP used as EU growth proxy.

Validation: blend CPI vs HICP = 0.98 corr, blend GDP vs EU GDP = 0.97 corr.
